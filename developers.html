Developer Portal

This developer portal provides technical information for software engineers and product managers to comprehend and adopt the solution. We welcome our community to send us feedback to enhance these documents.

Loopring

Loopring is a layer-2 protocol that consists of multiple smart contracts to verify tradable orders and perform per-trade settlement on-chain while keeping all order management off-chain. Loopring features a more generic design with matching-as-a-service, ring-matching, and dual-authoring technologies. We strongly encourage you to read our new whitepaper and previous blog posts as it is the best way to understand the protocol. If you have any questions, you can send us an email, or post questions on StackOverflow or Reddit. You can also learn more about the Loopring Protocol here.

Our Solution

There is a significant lack of security, transparency, and liquidity in centralized exchanges. Loopring invented a whole new trading experience built upon blockchain and smart contract technologies. Through our whitepaper and this developer portal, you will see that Loopring DEXs are non-custodial and perfectly secure; all trades are verified and recorded on the blockchain for absolute transparency, and every Loopring order can be shared with as many parties as possible with no trust needed, which also makes for higher liquidity and a better trade price.

The Loopring Foundation provides open-sourced, ready-to-deploy solutions for our community to develop the DEX frontend with apps (a.k.a wallets), the DEX backend (a.k.a relayers or match-engines), along with liquidity sharing middleware. We also provide free-of-charge consulting and technical support.

We have multiple patents in the US, the European Union, and China to protect the design and implementation of the Loopring Protocol. Before deploying a trading protocol on any public blockchain, please make sure your smart contracts don't include the ring-matching and dual-authoring features; otherwise, you must obtain our formal authorization for using our patents in writing.

Challenges

All DEX solutions suffer low TPS (transactions per second) and long block-time imposed by the underlying blockchains. Ethereum's current TPS is about 15 and block-time is roughly 15 seconds. Without a higher TPS, Loopring's trading experience won't be a match for centralized exchanges; but once Ethereum's TPS scales to 1000 or above, we believe the overall DEX trading experience will even better than centralized exchanges.

Loopring and all DEX protocols are not designed for high-frequency trading. This is not an issue at all, as we believe high-frequency trading is not fair for regular traders and has been dramatically misused for market manipulation of crypto prices.

Loopring vs. Kyber Network

Loopring and Kyber are designed for different use cases and are complementary to each other. Loopring features third-party matching-as-a-service (MAAS) whereas Kyber is a market-making protocol where market-makers offer their clients liquidity at promised prices within a given period. Kyber is more comparable to the Bancor protocol.

Loopring vs. 0x

Loopring and 0x are both order-based exchange protocols. We share many common designs, including:

  • Both protocols enable order-based trading of ERC-20 tokens;
  • Orders are managed off-chain, but settlement transactions are performed on-chain through smart contracts;
  • Both protocols suffer low TPS and long block-time of the underlying Ethereum blockchain;
  • Both teams are very open and supportive.

But we are also very different in many other ways:

  • Loopring's order model is much simpler and more generalized - one unidirectional model fits all; whereas 0x has maker orders and taker orders;
  • Loopring features a ring-style matching that can put more than two orders in a ring for higher liquidity and a better price. Ring-matching is a more generalized trading scheme than the trading pair notation that is used by 0x;
  • Loopring's Dual-Authoring technology prevents its orders and rings from being stolen by middlemen and Ethereum miners; it also adds another level of control for liquidity/order sharing;
  • Loopring offers a lot more open-sourced software for its partners to quickly build a DEX at a smaller cost, including a JavaScript library, two web-based wallet/DEX's, an embeddable HTML5 component for mobile apps, an iOS wallet application, a distributed relay cluster with ring-matching capability, and a liquidity sharing middleware based on pub-sub messaging;
  • Loopring will continue to focus on ERC-20 and will not support ERC-721 (non-fungible) tokens; whereas 0x aims to become the protocol for trading everything. We believe a protocol needs to be simple, elegant, easy to understand, and extensible.

Become a DEX or a Wallex

Loopring can turn any application or web page into a DEX. A DEX retrieves and displays orders and trades and may show submit-order and cancel-order forms for users to manage their trading data. In our model, a DEX does not have access to users' private keys. If it does, we then call it a Wallex (Wallet + DEX). Orders generated by a DEX must be signed from within a wallet app such as Ledger Nano before being sent to a relayer.

The Loopring team provides an open-sourced DEX named Circulr and an open-sourced Wallex named Loopr. You can clone one of these projects to launch a web frontend easily for accessing Loopring's default relayer system. If you want to start from scratch, we also offer a JavaScript library called loopring.js for you to access any compatible backend relayer system.

Loopring brings a new stream of income to DEX's and Wallex's- 20% of matching fees will be rewarded to a DEX that helps users generate their orders. This portion of fees will be automatically transferred to each DEX and Wallex's fee-collecting address by the Loopring Protocol after each trade has been settled, guaranteed!

Frontends have the option to connect to one or multiple relayers, given that permission is granted. In the graph above, "Frontend 8" connects to only one dedicated relayer. This is more like the centralized exchange mode where liquidity is not shared, and there is a single point of relayer failure; "Frontend 7" connects to one dedicated relayer while also shares large orders to "Relayer D" to speed up trading; "Frontend 5" always broadcasts orders to "Relay D" and "Relay C" to maximize trading speed and minimize price spread; and "Frontend 3" evenly shares liquidity across two liquidity-sharing networks through "Relay B"and "Relay C".

Connecting and broadcasting users' orders to multiple relayers and the liquidity-sharing networks increase the likelihood of orders being fulfilled. The network topologies and business relations between frontends, relayers, and the liquidity-sharing networks are entirely up to each entity in the ecosystem; the Loopring Foundation doesn't impose any rules. For more information regarding DEX and Wallex, check out the Frontends page.

If you are not happy with only 20% profit sharing, you can also run your relayer cluster.

Become a Relayer

Relayers manage orders and trades, and offer the non-custodial order-matching services as options. Relayers can receive orders from as many frontends as possible without trusting any of them. This opens the door to build an even bigger liquidity pool than any centralized exchange.

Relayers get 80% of matching fees, but it doesn't come for free - Relayers have to pay Ether as gas for ring-settlement transactions on Ethereum and pay fiat money for cloud service.

For more information about Relayers, check out the Relayers page.

Sharing Liquidity

Besides liqudity sharing among different frontends using the same relayer, relayers can also share liquidity with each other by joining the same liquidity sharing network. The Loopring Foundation provides a pub-sub based implementation of such a network, but ecosystem partners can build their liquidity sharing network with their technology stack of choice - the Loopring Protocol does not have a standard for liquidity sharing.

Each liquidity-sharing network may have their own internal economic model to incentivize order producers and charge orderers, which is not part of the Loopring Protocol.

For more information about liquidity sharing, check out the Liquidity page.
